Renting in Greece: Complete Guide for Expats

Discover how to navigate Greece's unique rental market. Learn legal requirements, realistic costs, and tips for securing housing in Athens and beyond.

Published May 1, 2026 8 min read

Understanding Greece's Rental Market

Greece's rental market reflects the country's economic history and recovery trajectory. The financial crisis (2010-2015) significantly impacted housing costs and rental dynamics, creating advantageous conditions for renters seeking affordable Mediterranean living. The market is characterized by informal negotiations, flexible terms, and often underdocumented arrangements, particularly for private rentals.

Unlike Northern European markets emphasizing contracts and formality, Greek rental culture operates with more flexibility and personal relationships. This informal approach can be advantageous for quick arrangements but requires careful attention to documentation and agreements. The market has become increasingly formalized in major cities, with professional agencies expanding their presence in Athens and expanding tourist destinations.

Greece's location, Mediterranean lifestyle, visa programs (like the Golden Visa), and favorable taxation for expats have attracted increasing international interest. This has driven housing costs upward in Athens and popular island destinations, though Greece remains significantly more affordable than Northern European alternatives.

Greek Rental Law and Legal Framework

Greece's rental legislation is less comprehensive than Northern European models but provides important tenant protections under Law 4604/2019 (Housing Lease Law).

Lease Agreements

Greek residential leases must be in writing, though formal notarized contracts are not always required for private rentals. However, for legal protection and tax compliance, written agreements are essential. Lease terms are negotiable, with one-year leases being most common, though 2-3 year agreements are available. Longer leases often result in better rent rates and more favorable terms.

Rental Increases

Greek law permits annual rent increases, typically 1-2% indexed to inflation. However, informal agreements with private landlords may include handshake arrangements avoiding formal increases. Formal, registered contracts provide legal protection against unexpected dramatic increases.

Deposits and Guarantees

Landlords typically request one month's rent as deposit (sometimes two), held in various arrangements. Unfortunately, Greek landlords don't consistently hold deposits in separate accounts as in Northern European markets. Request a signed receipt acknowledging deposit receipt and specify conditions for return. Professional agencies provide better deposit protection than private landlords.

Tenancy Duration and Termination

Fixed-term leases generally cannot be terminated early except for valid grounds (landlord needs property for personal use). Month-to-month or indefinite arrangements require 30-60 days written notice from either party. Unlike more regulated European markets, Greek law provides less automatic renewal protection, making clear contractual terms essential.

Documentation Required for Renting in Greece

Greece's documentation requirements vary depending on your nationality and employment status:

Essential Documents

  • Passport or ID: Valid passport or EU/EEA national identity card
  • Proof of Income: Employment contract or recent payslips
  • Bank References: Bank statements demonstrating financial stability (optional but helpful)
  • Previous Landlord Reference: If available, particularly valuable in competitive markets
  • AFM Number: Tax identification number (for formal contract registration)

For Non-EU Citizens

Non-EU citizens need valid visa or residency documentation. Greece's updated visa policies facilitate extended stays for remote workers and entrepreneurs, making housing more accessible. Some landlords prefer EU citizens due to perceived stability, but this discrimination is declining in Athens and tourist areas where international residents are common.

Getting Your AFM (Tax Number)

Greeks and residents need an AFM (Αριθμός Φορολογικού Μητρώου) for formal contracts and tax compliance. Non-residents can obtain temporary AFM through their local tax office (Δημόσια Δ.Ο.Υ.) or rental agencies can facilitate. This process takes a few days and provides legal formality to rental arrangements.

Rental Costs Across Greece

Greece offers some of Europe's most affordable housing, though prices vary significantly by location and market dynamics:

Athens (Capital)

Athens hosts most international expats and commands the highest rental prices. One-bedroom apartments in central areas (Syntagma, Kolonaki, Plaka) cost EUR 600–850 monthly, dropping to EUR 450–600 in outer neighborhoods (Marousi, Glyfada). Two-bedroom central apartments range from EUR 900–1,200. Working-class neighborhoods like Kaisariani and Vyronas offer excellent value (EUR 400–550) while maintaining quick metro access to city center.

Thessaloniki (Northern Hub)

Greece's second city offers 25-30% lower prices than Athens. One-bedroom apartments in central areas cost EUR 450–650, dropping to EUR 350–500 peripherally. Two-bedrooms range from EUR 650–900 centrally. Thessaloniki attracts young professionals and expats seeking Mediterranean lifestyle at significantly lower cost than the capital.

Island Destinations

Greek islands exhibit dramatic seasonal variation. Winter rentals are exceptionally affordable: Crete EUR 400–600 for one-bedrooms, Santorini EUR 500–700, Mykonos EUR 600–850. Summer tourism drives island prices dramatically higher, making winter the ideal renting season. Island living requires acceptance of seasonal unemployment and reduced services but offers extraordinary lifestyle benefits.

Smaller Cities and Rural Areas

Cities like Patras, Larissa, and Rethymno offer one-bedroom apartments for EUR 300–450. These areas attract remote workers and retirees seeking minimal living costs combined with Mediterranean climate. Internet infrastructure has improved significantly, supporting digital nomads increasingly choosing Greek smaller cities.

Regional Differences and Living Considerations

Greece's diverse geography creates distinct living experiences and economic patterns worth understanding before committing to a location.

Urban vs. Rural Divide

Athens and Thessaloniki offer cosmopolitan amenities, employment opportunities, healthcare, and diverse social communities. Smaller cities and islands provide authentic Mediterranean lifestyles, stronger community bonds, and dramatically lower costs. The trade-off involves reduced employment options, occasional service disruptions, and less diverse international communities outside major cities.

Seasonality Effects

Greek islands and coastal areas experience dramatic seasonality. Winter (November-March) offers peaceful living, lower costs, and authentic community atmosphere but reduced services and employment. Summer tourism peaks (June-August) bring vibrant nightlife and activities but overwhelmed infrastructure and inflated prices. Spring and fall provide ideal balance of weather, population, and services.

Infrastructure and Services

Athens and major cities offer modern infrastructure, reliable utilities, and comprehensive services. Smaller towns and islands may experience occasional water shortages, irregular electricity supply, and limited service availability. Inquire specifically about utility reliability before committing to off-season island living.

Tips for Successfully Renting in Greece

Obtain Your AFM Early

Get your tax identification number (AFM) from local tax authorities before signing formal leases. This facilitates contract registration and establishes legal residency. The process is straightforward; visit your local ΔΟΑΡ (tax office) with your passport and proof of residence.

Use Written Agreements Always

Even informal private rentals should include written agreements specifying rent amount, deposit terms, maintenance responsibilities, and termination conditions. This protects both parties and prevents misunderstandings that are difficult to resolve through Greek legal channels.

Get Deposit Receipts

Insist on signed receipts acknowledging deposit receipt from private landlords. Specify return conditions (within 30 days of lease termination, deductions for documented damages only). Professional agencies provide better protection; consider premium charges worthwhile for deposit security.

Understand Utility Arrangements

Clarify whether utilities (electricity, water, heating) are included in rent or tenant responsibility. Summer electricity costs are modest (EUR 30–50 monthly), but winter heating can exceed EUR 80–120 for older, inefficiently insulated apartments. Newer apartments offer better efficiency and lower costs.

Budget for Maintenance Flexibility

Greek rental properties often feature older infrastructure. Water heaters may be individual in-unit systems requiring maintenance; plumbing occasionally needs repair. Budget EUR 50–100 monthly contingency for unexpected maintenance, or negotiate maintenance responsibility specifics before signing.

Navigate Language Barriers

While English is increasingly common in Athens and among younger Greeks, many landlords and service providers speak limited English. Consider hiring a translator for formal contract review (USD 100–150) to prevent costly misunderstandings. Several apps provide real-time translation assistance.

Be Flexible on Arrangements

Greek rental culture embraces negotiation and flexibility. Don't accept first offers as final; propose modifications to rent, lease term, included utilities, or move-in dates. Many landlords are willing to negotiate, particularly for long-term reliable tenants.

How Greek Rental Agencies Assist Renters

Professional Greek real estate agencies (μεσιτικές γραφείες) are increasingly important, particularly in Athens and popular destinations:

Property Selection and Matching

Agencies maintain property databases and match tenant preferences with available rentals. This saves significant time navigating informal markets and provides access to properties not widely advertised.

Contract Preparation and Legal Compliance

Professional agencies prepare written contracts complying with Greek law, protecting both parties and establishing clear rental terms. This formality prevents future disputes and provides legal recourse if disagreements arise.

Deposit Protection

Many agencies hold deposits in dedicated accounts or through guarantee organizations, providing protection unavailable with private landlords. This security is worth any commission charged by professional agencies.

Ongoing Support and Dispute Resolution

Agencies provide ongoing management services, addressing maintenance issues and resolving landlord-tenant conflicts professionally. This intermediary role prevents direct confrontation and ensures responsive problem-solving.

Finding Your Greek Home

Greece's rental market rewards those who invest time exploring options and building relationships with local agents. Start with Greek rental agencies, supplement with local online platforms (Spiti, XE.gr), and don't hesitate to contact Athens rental agencies for personalized assistance. Many international relocation services specialize in Greek housing and can provide valuable guidance.

Greece offers exceptional lifestyle value for those willing to embrace Mediterranean culture and accept more informal arrangements than Northern European markets. By understanding local legal frameworks, gathering necessary documentation, using professional agencies for important rentals, and maintaining flexibility, you'll successfully navigate Greece's unique rental market and establish your home in this extraordinary country.
